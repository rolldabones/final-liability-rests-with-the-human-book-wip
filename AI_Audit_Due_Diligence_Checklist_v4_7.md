*Risk-Tiered Control Framework \| EU AI Act Article 6 Classification \| Deployment Context-Sensitive \| Version 4.7, Final*

11 domains \| 187 audit and due diligence questions \| 3 EU AI Act risk tiers \| 5 deployment contexts \| Synchronized with the current AI GRC Master Reference

## How to Use This Document

**As an audit instrument.** Each question maps to a Yes / No / Partial / Not Applicable finding, with evidence recorded in the Status / Remarks column. Section 0 scopes the audit universe before substantive testing begins. Sections 1 through 10 follow the sequence of governance, design, deployment and operation. Risk tier and deployment context tags allow auditors to scope the applicable question set to the specific system under review: a High-Risk, Regulated Sector system warrants the full set; a minimal-risk internal tool may require only the All Tiers rows. This document does not substitute for an audit plan, sampling methodology or evidence matrix. Those remain the auditor's responsibility. Final Liability rests with the Human.

**As a due diligence instrument.** Three DD contexts apply. Transactional (M&A, investment, joint venture): begin with Section 0 on regulatory investigations, enforcement history and prior incident disclosure, then Section 1 governance and supply chain. Vendor and third-party: focus on Sections 3, 4 and 5 (security, explainability and TEVV) and Section 10 (agentic governance where relevant). Regulatory readiness ahead of an EU AI Act market surveillance inquiry: focus on Sections 0, 1 and 6 (registration, QMS, FRIA and serious incident reporting). In all contexts, the Status / Remarks column should record the finding, the evidence source and the name of the person who provided it.

**As a self-assessment and stress-testing instrument.** Organizations building or maturing their AI governance, risk management and compliance systems can use this document to stress-test their frameworks before an external audit or regulatory inquiry arrives. Running the question set internally surfaces control weaknesses, policy gaps and unassigned ownership at lower cost than discovering them in the field. Risk tier and deployment context tags allow governance teams to prioritize remediation effort: High-Risk, Regulated Sector gaps first. The Status / Remarks column functions as a remediation tracker in this mode. Periodic self-assessments against this document generate the longitudinal improvement record that regulators and counterparties expect to see.

**Scope and limitations.** This document is a practitioner governance tool, not legal advice. It is aligned to the EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689), GDPR, ISO/IEC 42001:2023, NIST AI RMF 1.0, the IMDA Model AI Governance Framework for Agentic AI and the revised EU Product Liability Directive (Directive 2024/2853) as of the date of this version. Regulatory references must be verified against current law before reliance. All conclusions derived from this checklist must be reviewed by qualified legal and compliance professionals before adoption or external disclosure.

**Relationship to the AI GRC Master Reference and the synthesizing literature.** This Checklist is the operational instantiation of the control space described in the current AI GRC Master Reference. The Master Reference, at Part 25, integrates two synthesizing reference works from the Center for Security and Emerging Technology (CSET): the harmonized framework distilling 7,741 recommendations from 52 guidance documents into 258 practices across 5 categories and 34 topic areas (Crichton, Reddy, Ji, Crawford, Hoffmann, Shea-Blymyer and Bansemer, September 2025; doi 10.51593/20240041), and the operational reference mapping that framework to 232 action-subject pairs across an 18-stage AI adoption lifecycle from Self-Assess through Improve (Crichton, Reddy and Ji, April 2026; doi 10.51593/20250002). Auditors and self-assessors who want the literature-side view of the same control space should consult Master Reference Part 25 alongside this Checklist. Master Reference Part 25 covers what the literature says; this Checklist covers what the auditor asks. Both are required for a defensible audit posture; neither substitutes for the other. The current AI GRC Master Reference also covers, at Part 26, three conceptual and methodological reference works underlying the assurance discipline: the UK Department for Science, Innovation and Technology Introduction to AI Assurance (February 2024); the Goal Structuring Notation Community Standard, Version 3 (Safety-Critical Systems Club Assurance Case Working Group, May 2021); and Raji et al., Closing the AI Accountability Gap (FAT\* 2020). Auditors should consult Part 26 for the conceptual scaffolding (Trust, Trustworthiness, Justified Trust; the assurance-case structure; the SMACTR internal-audit framework) that this Checklist operationalizes.

<td><p><strong>EU AI Act Risk Tiers (Checklist Column)</strong></p>
<p><strong>High-Risk (Art. 6 + Annex III)</strong></p>
<p>Mandatory controls for systems in: biometrics, critical infrastructure, education/vocational, employment/HR, essential services (credit/insurance), law enforcement, migration/border control, justice and democratic processes. Full provider/deployer obligation stack applies once the high-risk requirements enter application; confirm the current schedule against the primary source.</p>
<p><strong>Limited Risk (Art. 50): practitioner shorthand</strong></p>
<p>Note: 'Limited Risk' is practitioner shorthand. The EU AI Act does not formally use this label. The Act's own classification is: prohibited (Art. 5), high-risk (Art. 6 + Annex III), transparency obligations (Art. 50) and minimal risk (ungoverned). Applies to: chatbots/conversational AI, emotion recognition and biometric categorization, deepfake generation and AI-generated public interest text. Obligations apply regardless of internal impact classification. Audits conducted before the Art. 50 obligations enter application should treat Art. 50 questions as gap-assessment and pre-implementation review. Fine: up to EUR 15M or 3% of worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.</p>
<p><strong>All Tiers</strong></p>
<p>Baseline controls applicable regardless of EU AI Act classification. Minimal-risk systems satisfy obligations at this tier.</p>
<p><strong>Internal Impact Classification (separate from EU AI Act tiers)</strong></p>
<p>Organizations must also assign an internal impact classification (High / Medium / Low Impact) to each AI system. This is distinct from the EU AI Act risk tier. A system may be internally classified as High Impact without falling within EU AI Act Annex III, or may be EU AI Act high-risk without being the highest-priority system on the internal risk register. Both classifications must be recorded in the AI system inventory for each system.</p></td>
<td><p><strong>Deployment Context Tags</strong></p>
<p><strong>General:</strong> Baseline: applies to all deployment contexts.</p>
<p><strong>Autonomous Decision-Making:</strong> AI makes or directly drives consequential decisions with limited human review (approvals, scoring, triage). HITL or HOTL oversight mandatory.</p>
<p><strong>Customer-Facing:</strong> Direct interaction with or decisions affecting external individuals. Heightened explanation, disclosure and appeal obligations apply.</p>
<p><strong>Regulated Sector:</strong> Finance, healthcare, legal, insurance, law enforcement, immigration. Sector-specific regulatory overlay applies. Legal review required before deployment.</p>
<p><strong>Agentic / GPAI:</strong> Orchestrated multi-step or multi-model systems; GPAI models with systemic risk under EU AI Act Art. 51 (training compute &gt; 10^25 FLOPs or Commission designation). Prompt injection and principal hierarchy governance required.</p>
<p><strong>Actor Classification (EU AI Act)</strong></p>
<p>Provider: places an AI system on the market or into service under its own name or trademark (Art. 3(3)). Bears full technical conformity and documentation obligations.</p>
<p>Deployer: uses an AI system under its own authority (Art. 3(4)). Bears oversight, log retention, input data and FRIA obligations where applicable.</p>
<p>Art. 25 Alert: A deployer that modifies a system or deploys it outside its intended purpose may become a Provider with the full provider obligation stack. This threshold must be assessed and documented before any modification.</p></td>
