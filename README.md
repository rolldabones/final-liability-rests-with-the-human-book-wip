## What this is

When AI removes the coordination work that white-collar institutions were built around, it does not close the gap between deployment and defensible governance. It reveals it. This book is about the human standing in that gap: the named person who authorized a deployment, who has to understand what they authorized and who will carry the consequence no matter how many automated layers sit between the authorization and the outcome.

It is a practitioner's book. It rests on thirty-five years of governance, risk management and compliance work across law, in-house counsel, trade compliance and risk management, and four years of daily AI practice on top of that. It argues for governed AI built like an honest tool rather than a magic trick. Human-made, for Easy Work, Simply Done.

The book is released free and for non-commercial use.

## The argument in three doctrines

**Slow AI.** Not slow operation and not a refusal to use AI. The discipline of placing inspection at the points where the system crosses a governance boundary, and running at speed everywhere else.

**Informed Intent.** The condition under which the authorizing human genuinely understands what they are approving, before they approve it, rather than receiving a briefing optimized for their confidence.

**Final Liability.** A named human carries the outcome of every AI deployment, regardless of how many layers stand between the authorization and the consequence.

The three are not separable. Slow AI produces the evidence that makes Final Liability assignable. Final Liability creates the incentive to build the governance Slow AI requires. Informed Intent closes the gap the other two leave open.

## How the book is organized

The manuscript runs to twenty-two chapters across six parts, with a Conclusion and an Epilogue.

- Part I, The Problem. Chapters 1 to 5.
- Part II, The Doctrine. Chapters 6 to 9.
- Part III, The Principal Was Always Human. Chapters 10 to 16.
- Part IV, The Contract Was Always the Workflow. Chapters 17 and 18.
- Part V, The Evidence Problem. Chapters 19 to 21.
- Part VI, The Governance Test. Chapter 22.
- Conclusion and Epilogue.

Appendices D, E, F, H, K and L are included in the manuscript. They are working instruments rather than supplements: an assumption register, an agent registry, a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment template, the Why Slow AI essay, the Berkeley Adopter's Checklist and the GRC next doctrine. Appendices I (AI Audit and Due Diligence Checklist), J (AI GRC Master Reference) and M (GRC Workbook) are maintained as separate documents.

## How it was written

This book argues that AI must be used with discipline, with Informed Intent and with a named human carrying Final Liability for what it produces. It was written that way. The author worked heavily with AI to draft, structure, stress-test and refine the manuscript, under intent specifications he wrote and against evidentiary standards he set. Every chapter and every line passed through his judgment before it appeared. The AI was a tool. The doctrine governing its use, and the responsibility for the result, are his.

A short song that compresses the three doctrines, with lyrics by the author and an AI-generated rendering, is linked in the Preface.

## A note on accuracy

The book leans on doctrine that does not depend on which regulation is in force this season. Where it cites current law, the regulatory and case claims were independently verified, most recently in the June 2026 revision. Time-sensitive legal points still move, so confirm any specific obligation against current sources before relying on it. Nothing here is legal advice, and reading it creates no attorney-client relationship.
